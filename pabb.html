The goal of meditation: PABB

Four practices that meditation trains — Pain, Abstinence, Barriers, and Boredom.

Meditation isn't an escape from life — it's rehearsal for it. Sitting with whatever arises builds four capacities that map neatly onto the acronym PABB. Each one turns something we usually avoid into a source of strength.

P

Pain — focus on it first

Turn toward discomfort instead of away from it.

The mind's default is to flinch from pain — physical, emotional, the ache of a hard truth. Meditation teaches you to focus on it first: to observe a difficult sensation or feeling with steady, curious attention. What you can sit with, you can understand; what you understand stops running your life from the shadows.

A

Abstinence — from unhealthy, addictive behaviors

Create a gap between urge and action.

Every craving is a wave: it rises, peaks, and passes if you let it. By watching an impulse without immediately obeying it, meditation builds the pause where choice lives. That pause is how you abstain from the addictive, not-healthy behaviors that hijack attention — the endless scroll, the reflexive snack, the numbing habit.

B

Barriers — the obstacle is the way

After Ryan Holiday's The Obstacle Is the Way.

Drawing on Stoic philosophy, Ryan Holiday argues that the impediment to action advances action — "the obstacle is the way." Meditation is daily practice at meeting barriers with composure: each wandering thought, each restless minute is an obstacle you calmly turn into the path itself. The block becomes the route.

"The obstacle in the path becomes the path. Never forget, within every obstacle is an opportunity to improve our condition."
— Ryan Holiday, The Obstacle Is the Way
B

Boredom — the trigger for creativity

Let the empty space do its work.

We reach for stimulation the instant boredom appears — and kill the very state where new ideas are born. Meditation makes friends with the empty space. When you stop filling every gap, the mind wanders productively, connects distant dots, and surprises you. Boredom, tolerated, becomes the trigger for creativity.
