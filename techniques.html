Meditation techniques

Three reliable ways in. Try each — keep whichever quiets your mind best.

🔍 The body scanner

Sweep attention slowly through the body like a scanner moving from head to toe. Rest on each region — scalp, face, shoulders, arms, chest, belly, legs, feet — noticing whatever sensation is there without trying to change it. Where you find tension, breathe into it and let it soften on the exhale.

Why it works: it anchors a busy mind in direct physical sensation, pulling you out of thought and into the present body.

👁 The outside observer

Imagine stepping outside yourself and watching yourself sit — as if you were a calm witness a few feet away, or looking down from above. From there, observe your thoughts and feelings arising and passing, like weather moving across a sky. You are not the storm; you are the awareness watching it.

Why it works: this "witness" stance loosens identification with thoughts and emotions, revealing the spacious awareness behind them.

🌬 Focus on the breath

The simplest and most universal anchor. Rest attention on the natural breath — the cool air at the nostrils, or the rise and fall of the belly. Don't control it; just feel it. When the mind wanders (it will), gently return to the next breath. Counting breaths from one to ten, then starting over, can help.

Why it works: the breath is always available, always in the present, and moves with the nervous system — slowing it calms you.

"Feelings come and go like clouds in a windy sky. Conscious breathing is my anchor."
— Thich Nhat Hanh

Pair these with Ohmmm breathing and good posture, then start a session.
